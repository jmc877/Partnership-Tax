  • Here is a basic outline of the material covered on the slides and where you should focus your attention.
    • Slides 1-8 [10 Min]: Please review carefully the P/L diagrams that we've covered before that illustrate long and short exposures for assets and loans. They are especially useful to illustrate graphically hedging with FFOs and how FFOs can be used to create synthetic positions.
    • Slides 9-11 (B&M: 683-687) [6 Min]: Overview of financial risks faced by companies and instruments used to manage these risks. The Additional Readings cover this a bit. Also: what are valid reasons for a company to hedge its risks?
    • Slides 12-17 (B&M: 690; skim 691-693) [20 Min]: Def. of ForK, example of ForK for sale of Google shares, and payoffs to both parties to ForK. S17 shows how owning an asset long and entering into the short side of a ForK creates a riskless position (hedge).
    • Slides 18-25 (B&M: 693-695) [15 min]: S18, which derives the forward-spot parity, is the most important in the materials. S19-20 demonstrate how the forward price is enforced via arbitrage. Please make sure that you understand how arbitrage prevents mispricing in S20. S21-S25 highlight the challenge that derivatives pose for regulation (e.g., securities, banking, & tax): since a forward gives the same payoffs as owning the asset and borrowing, given either two of those inputs, you can create the third. The regulatory rules that apply to each of these inputs, however, may be quite different.
    • Slides 26-30 [8 min; B&M: 693-695]: We will only briefly discuss the effect on forward pricing in the case of an asset that pays income (e.g., dividends, interest). We will only present the results in order to hone your intuition. Query: If you have a long forward position in a stock that pays a dividend, should the forward price be higher or lower than the forward price on an indentical, non-dividend paying stock?
    • Slides 31-32 (B&M: skim 691-693;693-696 ) [15 min]: Cover forward pricing on commodities with no convenience yield but storage costs (gold, silver) and commodities with convenience yield and storage costs (corn, oil). You can think of storage costs as negative income--when you hold the commodity, you have to pay the storage costs, but if you hold a forward contract you avoid the costs. Convenience yields are like income: if you hold the underlying you can consume it or hold it. For S32, ignore the formula and focus on the intuition.
    • Slides 33-34 [6 min]: Slide 33 is important. After you enter into a forward contract and the price of the underlying moves, the value of the contract will change: it increases for one party and decreases in an equal amount for another party. The slide shows how to calculate this value.
    • Slides 35-39 (B&M: skim 717-719; 720-722 and focus on interest rate parity) [15 min]: FX Forwards. You can think of currency as an asset that pays income, i.e.,the risk-free rate. We basically have the same pricing relationship as a dividend paying stock.
    • Slides 40-42 (B&M: 691-693) [10 min]: Futures are exchange traded forwards with certain contractual provisions that are designed to mitigate counter-party risk, e.g., marking-to-market.
    • Slides 43-48 (B&M: 698-700) [12 min]: We cover interest rate swaps. They are very easy if you first spend 5 minutes reviewing forward interest rates.
    • Slides 50-55: Companies can use interest rate swaps to create liability streams that are not dependent both on a company's credit rating AND interest rate risk.
    • Slides 56-57: Equity swaps--only if time

Derivatives (Part II): Options

  • Understanding the basics of option pricing is important for future corporate lawyers. Slides
  • You should understand: (1) the difference between calls and puts and how option payoffs are different than forward K payoffs; (2) put-call parity; (3) how options can be combined to create virtually any payoff (4) the bounds on option values and the effect on option prices when volatility, interest rates, the price of the underlying, and the option's term change; and (5) basic option pricing models, such as Black-Scholes.
